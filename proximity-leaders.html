The Setup
IF PROXIMITY MATTERS,
WHY DON'T THE
PROXIMITY LEADERS WIN?

In the 2025 PGA Tour season, Isaiah Salinda led proximity from 50–75 yards. Takumi Kanaya led from 75–100 yards rough. Pontus Nyholm led from 100–125 yards. Carson Young led the comprehensive 50–125 yard band. Beau Hossler led from 125–150 yards. Austin Eckroat led 150–175 yards.

Between them: two victories.

Meanwhile the 2025 World Golf Rankings read: Scheffler, McIlroy, Fleetwood, Schauffele, Henley, MacIntyre. None of them led a single proximity distance band. Scheffler won five times. McIlroy won three including the Masters. Fleetwood won the Tour Championship.

The Central Argument

"This is not a paradox. It is exactly what the data predicts — once you understand what proximity band leadership actually measures."

A category stat is not a performance stat. They are fundamentally different things — and confusing them is where most golf analysis goes wrong. The 2025 DataGolf dataset of 421 players and 5 SG categories makes this distinction impossible to ignore.

The Core Finding
ONE NUMBER RULES THEM ALL
The Question We Asked

We ran a simple test across 407 players in the DataGolf database: which single skill most reliably predicts whether a player wins?

We tested all five skills the PGA Tour tracks — approach play, putting, driving, short game, and all five combined. The answer is clear: no single skill predicts winning nearly as well as all five together.

Framework: The Five-Tool Concept

In baseball, a "five-tool player" excels at hitting for average, power, speed, fielding, and arm strength. Golf Digest applied the same concept to golf using PGA Tour strokes-gained data — a player earns a "tool" in each category where they gain 0.25+ strokes per round above average.

In 2025, only one player on tour qualified as a five-tool player: Scottie Scheffler. He won five times. We applied the same framework to the DataGolf performance CSV — same five categories, our own calculations directly from the data.

The sum of all five skills is by far the best predictor of wins — 83% stronger than approach play alone. He won five times. The next closest players — McIlroy, Fleetwood — were four-tool players. They combined for six more wins. No proximity band leader broke three tools.

The weakest single predictor of winning. Matters at the margins but rarely the deciding factor in a full season.

The Proximity Band Leaders · What The Data Reveals
FOUR REASONS THEY LEAD
BANDS BUT NOT LEADERBOARDS

The six proximity leaders in our group don't share a single explanation — they represent four completely different problems. Understanding which applies to each player is the most useful thing the data tells us.

Reason #1
WRONG SKILL
GETS CREDITED
Beau Hossler

Hossler leads proximity from 125–150 yards. When you see that, you assume he has an elite iron game. The data says otherwise — his overall approach play ranks 302nd out of 407 players.

What's actually elite? His chipping and pitching around the green — ranked 10th on tour. He lays up to his comfortable yardage window, then uses that short-game brilliance to knock it close. The proximity stat captures the end result but misidentifies the cause. It looks like an iron play stat. It's actually a short game stat.

Reason #2
ONE BRIGHT
SPOT, LEAKING
EVERYWHERE ELSE
Isaiah Salinda

Salinda leads proximity from 50–75 yards. But zoom out and the full picture is stark: he ranks below tour average in every single other category.

His putting ranks 364th out of 407. His overall score is negative — he costs himself strokes relative to the field. DataGolf's expected wins model says he should win once every 20 seasons. One bright spot doesn't overcome four problem areas. Short wedge proximity is real, but it's doing nothing for his scorecard because everything surrounding it is broken.

Reason #3
WINS THE
BATTLE, LOSES
THE WAR
Sam Ryder · Carson Young

Ryder's short wedge proximity is real — not a fluke. His putting ranks 23rd on tour. But he gets there the hard way.

His driving ranks 259th. When you can't hit fairways or generate distance, you arrive at your approach shots from rough, awkward distances, and under more pressure. Players who are elite off the tee hit the same short irons from perfect lies. Ryder is fighting uphill just to get to the shot he's good at — then executing it well but not building enough of a scoreboard advantage to matter.

Carson Young is the same pattern from a different angle: comprehensive proximity leadership but negative approach, negative short game, negative putting season-long. One band doesn't tell the story of a full round.

Reason #4
THE SAMPLE
SIZE TRAP
Kanaya · Nyholm

Some proximity band leadership is simply a small sample size problem dressed up as a season-long achievement.

Kanaya led proximity from 75–100 yards in the rough at 1 foot 8 inches. That sounds extraordinary — until you realize a player might hit 8 to 15 shots from that exact combination of distance and lie condition in an entire season. One excellent stretch — maybe one good week at a course that sets up that shot — can produce a season-leading average from a tiny handful of attempts.

His full-season approach play ranks 336th out of 407. Nyholm can't even be measured — his rounds weren't tracked by the ShotLink system that powers strokes-gained data. He won once in 2025. Without the data, no conclusions can be drawn about why.
